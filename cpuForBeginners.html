CPU for Beginners: Everything You Need to Know

Written by: The PC Parts Guide Team

March 2026 • 8 min read

The CPU — Central Processing Unit — is the single most important part of your computer. It's the like a boss that tells everyone what to do. Without it, your computer is just a box of metal and plastic that does nothing.

What Does a CPU Actually Do?

Think of the CPU as your computer's brain. Just like your brain tells your hands what to type, your mouth what to say, and your legs where to walk; the CPU tells every part of the computer what to do. So if there's no brain, you can't think. If there's no CPU, your computer can't do anything.

Modern CPUs have multiple cores, each capable of handling tasks independently. More cores allow the CPU to juggle more work at once, which is why they matter a whole lot for multitasking and production.

Important Numbers: Cores & GHz

Cores = Workers

You can think of cores as workers in a company or an office. More workers = more tasks can be done at the same time.

  • 2-4 cores: Basic office/school work, web browsing, and light gaming.
  • 6-8 cores: Great for gaming, and everyday use.
  • 10+ cores: Ideal for multitasking, video editing, streaming, and 3D rendering.
FOR GAMERS: 6-8 cores is the sweet spot for gaming. Most games don't normally use more than that. If you go above 8 cores, you won't see much improvement in gaming performance. That and a good graphics card will give you the best gaming experience. If you want to find out how to pick a GPU click here!

GHz = Speed

GHz (gigahertz) tells you how fast each core works. Think of it as how fast each worker can finish their tasks.

  • 3.0-4.0 GHz: Normal speed for everyday tasks. Gets the job done.
  • 4.0-5.0 GHz: Fast speed for gaming and content creation.
  • 5.0+ GHz: High-end CPUs can boost to these speeds, but it's not necessary for most users.
INFO: The base clock is the speed that your CPU will run under normal conditions. The boost clock is the maximum speed it can reach under light loads. For gaming, you should be eyeing a higher boost clock for better performance.
Better CPU = More Cores + Higher GHz

(But make sure both are balanced)

TDP (Thermal Design Power) = How Hot Your CPU Gets

TDP tells you how much heat your CPU generates while doing tasks. Higher TDP means a hotter CPU. A hot CPU will need better cooling to prevent it from slowing down (thermal throttling).

  • 65W or less: Low power, runs cool. A basic/stock cooling fan is fine. Good for Office PCs
  • 65-125W: Standard desktop CPUs, suitable for gaming and general use. Will need a decent fan for cooling.
  • 125W+: High-performance CPUs. Will need better cooling like liquid cooling.

Socket Compatibility = Make Sure it Fits

The socket is like a power plug. Different CPUs use different sockets, and your motherboard needs to have the right socket for your CPU.

  • Intel: LGA 1200 (10th/11th gen), LGA 1700 (12th gen+)
  • AMD: AM4 (Ryzen 1000-5000), AM5 (Ryzen 7000+)
IMPORTANT: Always check that your CPU's socket is compatible with your motherboard before purchasing. An Intel CPU will NOT fit in an AMD motherboard and vice versa.

AMD vs Intel — Does It Matter?

In 2026, both AMD and Intel still offer excellent value. The choice often comes down to specific models and pricing.

  • AMD Ryzen: Often better value for money. Great for gaming and mulittasking. AM4 motherboards are cheaper and more widely available.
  • Intel Core: Often better for pure gaming speed. Newer Intel CPUs offer improved efficiency and performance.

The truth is, it doesn't really matter which brand you pick as long as you chooose a CPU that fits your needs and budget. Both brands have strong options at every price point. Look at reviews and benchmarks for the specific models you're interested in to make the best choice.

Pro tip: Don't overspend on a CPU if you're gaming as most titles are GPU-limited. A mid-range CPU paired with a strong GPU will outperform an expensive CPU with a weak GPU every time.
