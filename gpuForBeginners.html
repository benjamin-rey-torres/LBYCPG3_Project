GPU for Beginners: Picking the Right Graphics Card

Written by: The PC Parts Guide Team

March 2026 • 8 min read

The GPU (Graphics Card) is what makes your games look beautiful and run smoothly. It's the most expensive and important part in most gaming PCs.

What Does a GPU Do?

The GPU is the artist. The CPU says to "draw a tree and a river," and the GPU does the heavylifting to paint every single leaf, every drop of water, every strand of grass, and every ray of light. Without a good GPU, everything will look blurry, pixelated, and ugly.

In a game running at 60 FPS (frames per second), your GPU is drawing 60 complete images every second. You need a powerful GPU to achieve that as drawing that many pictures that fast is incredibly demanding.

Modern GPUs also accelerate in AI tasks (like NVIDIA GPUs), video encoding/decoding, and scientific simulations, making them quite relevant beyond gaming.

The Important Specs

VRAM: The GPU's Memory

VRAM is like the GPU's desk space. It holds all the textures, colors, and 3D models it needs to draw what you see in your game.

  • 4GB VRAM: Okay for old games and low settings
  • 8GB VRAM: Good for 1080p gaming (most common)
  • 12GB VRAM: Great for 1440p gaming
  • 16GB+ VRAM: For 4K gaming and future-proofing
IMPORTANT: If you run out of VRAM, your game will STUTTER like crazy. For modern games at 1080p, get at least a GPU with 8GB of VRAM.

Resolution: How Clear is Your Picture?

Resolution is how many tiny dots (pixels) make up your screen. More pixels = clearer picture = harder work for GPU.

NVIDIA vs AMD: The Great Battle

It's like iPhone vs Android — both are great, but have different features and specialties.

  • NVIDIA RTX (Green Team):
    • DLSS: Makes games run faster without losing quality
    • Ray Tracing: Makes reflections and shadows look real
    • Better for video editing and streaming
    • Power efficient compared to matching AMD GPU
    • Usually more expensive
  • AMD RX (Red Team):
    • Better "bang for your buck" — more performance per peso
    • Usually has more VRAM for the price
    • FSR: Their version of DLSS
    • Often cheaper for the same performance
    • Performs worse than NVIDIA GPUs for editing, rendering, AI software, etc.

What About Intel?

Intel entered the GPU market with their Arc series. They're still new and not as powerful as NVIDIA or AMD, but they offer good value for budget builds and are improving rapidly. If you're on a tight budget, an Intel Arc card might be worth considering.

WHAT TO CHOOSE: Want ray tracing and best features? Go NVIDIA. Want the most FPS for your money? Go AMD. Both are amazing in their own right.

Memory Type: GDDR6 vs GDDR6X

GDDR6X is a faster variant of GDDR6 with higher bandwidth. It's found on NVIDIA's higher-end cards. For most gamers, the difference is only noticeable at 4K or in memory-bandwidth-heavy games. GDDR6 is excellent for 1080p and 1440p.

Don't Forget Your PSU (Power Supply Unit)

High-end GPUs draw a whole lot of power. Always leave headroom — if your GPU calls for 650W, pair it with an 850W unit for stability and future upgrades.

  • Budget GPU (RX 6600, RTX 3050): Needs 500W-550W PSU
  • Mid-range GPU (RTX 3060, RX 6700): Needs 600W-650W PSU
  • High-end GPU (RTX 4070, RX 7800): Needs 700W-750W PSU
  • Top-tier GPU (RTX 4080, RX 7900): Needs 850W+ PSU
SAFETY TIP: Always buy a good quality PSU from trusted brands (Corsair, Seasonic, EVGA). A cheap PSU can damage your expensive GPU so choose carefully!
Watch out for bottlenecks: Pairing a very powerful GPU with a weak CPU can cause a CPU bottleneck. The CPU can't give instructions fast enough, so your expensive GPU just sits around doing nothing.

Example: RTX 4090 (₱85,000) + Old i3 CPU (₱4,000) = WASTE OF MONEY!

Good matches:
• RTX 3060 → Ryzen 5 5600
• RTX 4070 → Ryzen 7 5700X / i5-13600K
• RTX 4080 → Ryzen 7 7800X3D / i7-13700K

Always check benchmarks for your specific CPU + GPU combo to avoid bottlenecks.
